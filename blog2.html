Fifth post: Books

Wednesday, December 23, 2020

I want to start by talking about books. More specifically, a list of some of my favorite books, as well as some books that I will be reading soon.

Read more...

Nassim Taleb’s “Fooled by Randomness: The Hidden Role of Chance in the Markets and in Life” and “The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable” are definitely worth the read. The two books discuss mathematics and philosophy (probability). Taleb’s fundamental concepts lie in the fact that we should expect the unexpected. An unexpected or highly improbable event (rare) that has a major effect and is inappropriately rationalized after the fact due to hindsight bias is referred to as a black swan event. Events such as the advent of the internet, 9/11, and WWI are examples of black swan events. Some people would argue that the novel COVID-19 pandemic was a black swan event, but it actually wasn’t. The COVID-19 pandemic could have been predicted, and even was numerous times. In his books, Taleb also discusses Monte Carlo simulations. Monte Carlo simulations are simulations that rely on repeated randomized trials (thousands) to solve an underlying problem. Monte Carlo simulations are great for understanding the impact of risk and uncertainty in prediction and forecast models as they take the average of the results and obtain an estimate. This type of simulation can be applied to various fields such as finance, science, and engineering, among many. A Monte Carlo Simulation differs from a historical simulation in that it does not rely on historical data to build future predictions. If you would like to learn more, read Taleb’s books here. Another book that I have enjoyed reading is “Something Deeply Hidden,” by Sean Carroll. This book discusses quantum physics, its applications, and other important principles. Seeing the words “quantum physics” may scare some people away, but learning about our current understanding of the fundamental nature of our universe is extremely appealing to me. I would not consider myself a math or physics servant, but Carroll does a great job of discussing extremely complex topics in layman’s terms. He makes his points clear and concise and makes learning about quantum physics enjoyable. I’d definitely recommend checking his book out. Some other books that are currently on my must-read list include “The 48 Laws of Power,” by Robert Greene, “The Sovereign Individual: How to Survive and Thrive during the Collapse of the Welfare State,” by James Dale Davidson and Lord William Rees-Mogg, Steven Pinker's “Enlightenment Now,” William Rosen's “The Most Powerful Idea in the World,” and “How Asia Works,” by Joe Studwell. As Naval Ravikant would say (and I am definitely paraphrasing), it is not about the quantity of books that you read, but the quality and substance of the information you are reading about. In other words, you can read 100 books in a row, but if you are not grasping any of the information you are reading, then what’s the point? On the other hand, you can read the same 5 books over and over again for the rest of your life, but as long as you are getting true meaning out of them, then that is worthwhile. Naval also says that it doesn’t matter if you read a book through completely, that it is completely OK to skip around in a book and read the topics that are most interesting or applicable to you. I love this mindset and have applied it to my own reading habits. I will admit, some books bore me, which is likely true for most people. So I like to read about things I am most interested in, and will not waste my time with topics that I do not care about. Capeesh?

I don’t want to drag out stimulus talk, but hopefully the two sides can agree on something that is more helpful for the millions of Americans that need it.
